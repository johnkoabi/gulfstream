Project Overview

Gulfstream Bays is a proposed 35-unit luxury auto condo development in Boynton Beach, Florida, targeting Palm Beach County's high-net-worth collector vehicle community. Each unit is 2,400 SF (1,800 SF ground floor + 600 SF mezzanine), delivered as a blank canvas with basic finishes, and sold fee-simple at a target price of ~$680/SF (~$1.63M per unit).

The site is assembled from five parcels on N Federal Highway — three commercially zoned, two residentially zoned — all currently owned by Blaque Ink LLC. The assemblage is unincorporated Palm Beach County for four parcels; one parcel (3908 N Federal) falls within the City of Boynton Beach.

Due Diligence — Six Go/No-Go Factors

During the 6-month free-look period, six critical items must be resolved. Failure on any single factor is grounds to exit and recover the $200K deposit in full. All six are currently open — none has been validated.

  • Open
    1 — Zoning / Entitlement for Fee-Simple Auto Condos
    Two of the five parcels are zoned RM (residential multi-family) and must be rezoned. The commercially zoned parcels span two jurisdictions (Unincorporated PBC + City of Boynton Beach) with two different zoning codes (CG and C3). Fee-simple auto condo as a use category may require a special exception, rezoning, or conditional use approval. This is the foundational go/no-go item and should be the first diligence task initiated — ideally with a land use attorney in week one.
  • Open
    2 — Site Plan Feasibility for 35 Units on 2.66 Acres
    35 units × 2,400 SF = 84,000 SF of building on ~2.66 acres. An architect / civil engineer must confirm this density is achievable with required setbacks, fire access lanes, stormwater, and parking. If the site maxes out at 28–30 units, revenue drops by $8–11M. This needs an early conceptual site plan — before investing heavily in other diligence.
  • Spec Defined
    3 — Lift Capacity: Structural Slab & Ceiling Height
    Research confirms the required specification for new construction: 6" slab at 4,000 PSI minimum with #4 rebar grid, plus thickened pads (8"+) at lift anchor point locations. This supports two-post lifts up to 12,000 lbs and four-post lifts up to 14,000 lbs — covering virtually all collector vehicles including heavy SUVs and exotics. For ceiling height, 16–18 ft clear is the target to allow a car on a four-post lift with comfortable clearance above. Since this is new construction, designing to this spec from day one costs minimal incremental and eliminates the issue entirely. Action: Write 6" / 4,000 PSI slab and 18 ft clear ceiling height into the structural program before GC pricing. This removes the question as a buyer objection before it arises.
  • Open
    4 — Validated Construction Cost (GC-Level Estimate)
    The $18,375,000 construction cost ($219/SF on 84,000 SF GBA) is the model's biggest exposed assumption. A pre-construction estimate or GC budgeting exercise is needed before the hard money date. This should encompass demolition of existing structures (~20,600 SF across 6 buildings), site work, the full structural and MEP build-out including mezzanines, and overhead door systems. If GC pricing comes in at $250–$300/SF, total project cost rises by $2.6M–$6.8M — reducing net profit from ~$20M toward ~$13–17M. Still viable, but the range matters significantly for investor returns and equity sizing.
  • Open
    5 — Demonstrated Buyer Demand / Pre-Sales
    Market pricing is validated by comps, but project-specific demand needs evidence. Target: refundable reservations, signed LOIs, or strong broker feedback with named prospects prior to the hard money date. Pre-sales also de-risk the construction financing conversation with lenders.
  • Open
    6 — Equity Investor Commitments
    ~$10.8M in equity must be soft-circled before the hard money date and committed before land closes. Investor conversations should begin immediately — not after diligence completes. LP/GP terms, preferred return, and promote structure need to be defined first so conversations are productive.

Key Risks & Open Issues

Entitlement & Site Risks

  • High
    Residential Parcel Rezoning (Lots 110 & 111)
    Two parcels are zoned RM (multi-family residential). Rezoning to commercial/industrial is required and is not guaranteed. If denied or conditioned unfavorably, the effective site area shrinks and the unit count must be revised downward.
  • High
    Dual Jurisdiction Complexity
    One parcel is in the City of Boynton Beach; four are in Unincorporated PBC. This may require approval from two different planning and permitting bodies, creating coordination risk and potential for conflicting requirements.
  • Medium
    Site Plan Density — 35 Units May Not Fit
    84,000 SF on 2.66 acres is a FAR of ~0.73 — achievable, but site coverage, setbacks, access lanes, and stormwater will all consume land. Each lost unit = ~$1.63M in gross revenue.

Financial Risks

  • High
    Construction Cost Overrun
    The model's most exposed assumption. Comparable South Florida commercial construction has been running $250–$300/SF post-COVID. A $50/SF overrun on 84,000 SF = $4.2M additional cost and reduces margin from 64% to ~57%. The project is still very profitable, but this variable needs validation before hard money.
  • Medium
    Land Cost Concentration (~49% of Total Cost)
    At $9M, land represents nearly half of all-in development cost. There is no flexibility on this line item once the purchase closes — all cost overruns and any revenue shortfalls flow directly to developer profit.
  • Medium
    Absorption Timeline Not Modeled
    The economics assume 100% sell-through but do not model timing. If 35 units take 36 months to sell rather than 18, carry costs increase and investor IRR compresses materially. An absorption schedule should be built into the model with conservative and optimistic scenarios.
  • Medium
    Feb 2027 Hard Closing Deadline
    If entitlement or financing takes longer than expected, the outside closing date creates forced decision-making pressure. The window between the hard money date and the closing deadline must be sufficient to complete equity raise and construction loan commitment.

Market Risks

  • Monitor
    Competitive Supply Entering Market
    CollectionSuites is actively selling; The Paddocks of Delray is brand new. If additional auto condo projects enter Palm Beach County during the development and sales timeline, pricing competition could emerge in what is still a nascent market.
  • Monitor
    Discretionary Purchase Sensitivity
    At $1.6M, this is a discretionary luxury purchase. UHNW buyers are less rate-sensitive but are not immune to equity market downturns or wealth effect contractions. The project is not underwriting against a specific macro scenario — but it's worth monitoring.

Resolved / De-Risked

  • Resolved
    Pricing Validated by Active Market Comps
    CollectionSuites ($699–$701/SF) and The Paddocks ($613/SF) bracket a market range. The $680/SF target is well-supported. This is the most important single validation in the entire model.
  • Resolved
    Blank Canvas Delivery Model Confirmed as Market Standard
    Both active comps deliver blank canvas spaces. Buyers prefer it. The ~15 LF kitchen / bath / epoxy spec is in line with or slightly below comp delivery standards — appropriately conservative for cost management.
  • Resolved
    Purchase Price Reduced: $9.5M → $9.0M
    The negotiated reduction from $9.5M to $9M saves $500K in land cost, improves margin modestly, and reduces the equity raise requirement by the same amount.
  • Resolved
    Deal Structure Provides Meaningful Downside Protection
    6-month free look, $200K deposit, hard money at month 6, close by Feb 2027. Maximum capital at risk during diligence = $200K. The structure is appropriate for the risk profile of the project.

Additional Open Questions

The following questions were identified through research and analysis during the diligence period. They are ranked in descending order of importance. Status reflects what has been determined to date.

  • Partially Resolved
    1 — FEMA Flood Zone & Site Elevation
    Finding: Zone AE, BFE = 8 ft NAVD88 confirmed via Palm Beach County GIS (effective and pending maps are identical — no change incoming). The flood zone boundary does not cover the full site — it clips only the southeast corner near the Federal Highway frontage. The majority of the site (rear parcels, Old Dixie side) appears to fall in Zone X (minimal risk) based on parcel overlay mapping.

    Elevation data: A USGS elevation profile of the site shows the ground rises from approximately 6.5 ft NAVD at the Federal Highway frontage to ~11 ft toward the rear — a ~4.5 ft grade change across roughly 200 ft of site depth. The Federal Highway edge is approximately 1.5 ft below the BFE of 8 ft.

    Implications: (1) The front portion of the site requires fill to bring finished floor to minimum BFE + freeboard, targeting ~9–10 ft finished floor elevation. If only the southeast corner clips AE, fill scope may be limited — budget $50–150K depending on area affected. (2) The site's natural slope from front to back must be resolved in the civil design: auto condos require a level threshold at every overhead door. A sloped apron or grade break cannot be tolerated for exotic vehicle access. A uniform finished pad elevation is the recommended approach. (3) Flood insurance will be required by any construction lender on a Zone AE site, adding $3,000–8,000/year to operating costs — include in HOA budget projections.

    Next step: Order a topographic survey and Elevation Certificate. Confirm exact AE boundary versus X boundary at the parcel level. The FEMA FIRMette PDF (from msc.fema.gov) for panel covering this site should be pulled and reviewed with the civil engineer.

    Potential additional cost (not in pro forma): Site fill and grading $50,000–$150,000 depending on scope confirmed by survey. Flood insurance on Zone AE buildings ~$4,000–$8,000/year (HOA line item).
  • Partially Resolved
    2 — Florida Condo Pre-Sale Deposit Law
    Finding: Florida Statutes §718.502 and §718.202 govern this directly. Key rules for a non-residential (auto) condo:

    (a) Refundable reservations are legal now — before the declaration is recorded and before binding contracts — as long as a reservation program is filed with the FL Division of Condominiums and the developer has a contractual interest in the land (which the current PSA provides). Reservation deposits must be escrowed with an independent agent and are immediately refundable on written request.

    (b) Binding purchase contracts cannot be executed until the developer files the full condominium documents with the Division and receives notification that the filing is proper.

    (c) For non-residential condos, deposits up to 10% of sale price must be escrowed, but the developer may substitute a surety bond or irrevocable letter of credit for some or all of the escrow requirement.

    Implication: A reservation campaign can begin immediately and legally. This is a significant tool — collecting 10–15 refundable reservations during the diligence period validates demand, de-risks the lender conversation, and costs nothing if the project doesn't proceed. Action: Engage a Florida condo attorney to set up the reservation program and escrow structure — this should happen in parallel with zoning diligence.
  • Open
    3 — Construction Financing / Lender Pre-Sale Requirements
    The South Florida construction lending market is active and lenders familiar with condo sellout structures exist (Builders Capital, Nelson Funding, Bank OZK, and regional private lenders all active in the market). No lender with specific auto condo experience has been identified, but this is unlikely to be a hard barrier — the sellout structure is analogous to residential condo construction loans. The more important constraint is pre-sale thresholds: most lenders in this space require 30–50% of units under signed contract before funding. At 35 units, that means 11–18 signed purchase agreements. This makes the reservation-to-contract pipeline a prerequisite for construction financing, not just a nice-to-have. Action: Lender conversations should begin during the diligence period, before the hard money date, to understand specific pre-sale requirements.
  • Open
    4 — HOA / Condo Association Structure & Monthly Dues
    A condo association must be formed and funded for the ongoing management of common areas, gating/security, landscaping, insurance on common elements, and reserve contributions. Monthly dues are a buyer carrying cost and will be a question in every sales conversation. Dues that are too high compress buyer affordability; dues that are too low create underfunded reserves. For a 35-unit auto condo with gated access, security cameras, landscaping, and common area maintenance, estimated dues are likely $400–$800/month. The condo documents (declaration, bylaws, budget) must be drafted as part of the condo filing process. Action: Include HOA structure and pro forma budget in the condo attorney engagement.

    Potential additional costs (not in pro forma): Condo declaration drafting and filing $8,000–$15,000 (legal). HOA setup costs $2,000–$5,000. These are one-time costs that may already be captured within the entitlement cost line.
  • Open
    5 — Environmental / Phase I ESA & Demolition Scope
    The site has six existing buildings totaling ~20,600 SF, constructed between 1949 and 1979. Two risks require investigation: (1) Asbestos-containing materials (ACM) are virtually certain in structures of this age — floor tiles, pipe insulation, roofing materials, and joint compounds commonly contained asbestos pre-1980. An asbestos survey and abatement plan are required before demolition and must be budgeted separately from general demolition. (2) Underground storage tanks (USTs) and petroleum contamination — the Federal Highway corridor in South Florida has a well-documented history of UST contamination from gas stations and auto-related uses. A Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (ESA) will flag any known or suspected contamination from adjacent or prior uses. If a Phase II (soil/groundwater sampling) is triggered, timeline and cost could be material. Action: Order Phase I ESA immediately — it is standard due diligence and should be completed within the first 60 days of the diligence period. Budget $3,500–$6,000 for Phase I; asbestos survey additional $2,000–$4,000.

    Potential additional costs (not in pro forma): Phase I ESA $3,500–$6,000. Asbestos survey $2,000–$4,000. ACM abatement prior to demolition $20,000–$80,000 depending on scope. Phase II (if triggered) $15,000–$40,000. Remediation, if required, $50,000–$500,000+ — the tail risk item to screen for early.

    6 — Overhead Door Sizing for Exotic / Wide-Body Vehicles
    Standard commercial roll-up doors are typically 10 ft wide × 10 ft tall — adequate for most vehicles but tight for wide-body exotics (Lamborghini Urus, Ferrari Roma Spider with mirrors, lifted trucks) and insufficient for boats or large RVs. For a luxury auto condo targeting collectors, the minimum door specification should be 12 ft wide × 14 ft tall, with 14 ft × 14 ft preferred for the broadest vehicle compatibility. Door sizing affects structural header design and unit width, so this must be defined in the architectural program before construction drawings. This is a spec decision, not a discovery item — but it needs to be made explicitly and documented before GC pricing. A door that's too narrow is a defect that cannot be corrected post-construction.

    7 — Electrical Capacity: 200A Service per Unit + EV Charging
    Each unit will require significant electrical load: two-post lift motor (~3A at 220V), HVAC (10–20A), LED lighting, receptacles, and increasingly, EV charging. A Level 2 EV charger (32A at 240V) is now a baseline expectation for the UHNW buyer demographic — many will have multiple EVs or hybrids. The electrical design must confirm: (a) 200A minimum service per unit with panel sized for lift, HVAC, and two EV charger circuits; (b) conduit stub-out for EV charging included in base spec even if chargers are buyer-supplied; (c) utility transformer capacity — 35 units at 200A each is 7,000A aggregate demand. The utility (FPL) must confirm available capacity at this location and any upgrade costs. Utility capacity constraints are a project risk that is often discovered late. Action: Contact FPL during diligence to confirm transformer availability.

    Potential additional costs (not in pro forma): FPL transformer upgrade if required $20,000–$100,000 (utility-dependent). Conduit rough-in for EV charging at all 35 units $1,500–$2,500/unit = $52,500–$87,500 — recommend including in base construction scope.

    8 — Title Review: Easements, Deed Restrictions & Encumbrances
    A five-parcel assemblage assembled across multiple ownerships and jurisdictions commonly carries easements, access restrictions, utility easements, or deed restrictions that can affect the site plan or limit uses. A preliminary title search on all five parcels should be ordered early in the diligence period. Key items to flag: any recorded easements crossing the site (utility, drainage, access), any deed restrictions that limit use to specific categories, any liens or encumbrances on the Blaque Ink LLC ownership, and any cross-parcel agreements between the five lots. Easements discovered post-contract can affect unit layout, density, and access — better to know now.

    9 — Stormwater & Impervious Surface Requirements
    An auto condo development is effectively 100% impervious — buildings, drive aisles, and parking leave minimal pervious surface on a 2.66-acre site. South Florida stormwater regulations (SFWMD and local) require retention or detention for a design storm event. On a tight urban infill site, stormwater management can consume a meaningful percentage of land area through retention ponds, dry wells, or underground exfiltration systems. Stormwater requirements also interact directly with the flood zone designation (Zone AE) and finished floor elevation requirements. This needs to be evaluated by the civil engineer as part of the early site plan feasibility work — it is a potential constraint on unit count if the retention footprint is large.

    10 — Resale Liquidity / Secondary Market for Auto Condos
    The auto condo asset class is growing nationally — projects are active or recently launched in Fort Myers, Lakewood Ranch, Auburndale, Palm Beach, and Delray Beach — but it remains nascent enough that secondary market resale data is essentially nonexistent in this submarket. CollectionSuites and The Paddocks are both too new to have resale comps. Buyers at $1.6M will ask: "What is this worth in five years and how easily can I sell it?" The honest answer today is: unknown. The market trajectory is positive (growing collector car market, increasing demand, limited new supply), but liquidity risk is real and should be disclosed clearly in the offering. One approach is to proactively build a buyer community that creates its own secondary market liquidity — a waitlist of interested buyers to whom the developer facilitates resales. This is worth building into the marketing strategy from day one.
